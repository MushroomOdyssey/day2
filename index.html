Today we I will explore a number of HTML elements that we use to add formatting and semantic meaning to our webpages.

But before I get into all of this, let's define the word semantic. A quick dictionary search reveals the following definition: relating to meaning in language or logic. That's rather vague, isn't it? Let's explore that word a little more thoroughly so we can gain an appreciation of its impact in our lives and in our HTML.

When I write the word "fluffy", and you read it, there's a lot that's actually happening in that interaction. You see a collection of symbols on a page that come together to represent a series of noises that we would make to each other if we were standing face-to-face. But... it's more than that, right? The word has a meaning outside of what we see and hear. It conveys something that we feel, that we imagine, even. All of those internal processes that instantly spring into action and present you with thoughts of furry animals, soft tactile sensations, and plump shapes provide the actual meaning to the word, as you perceive it.

That's one of the reasons we have so many minor variations of a concept - think fluffy vs fuzzy vs floofy vs frizzy vs puffy, etc. I'd bet that if you had to generate a basic visualization of each of those words, they'd look extremely similar. Each word is more than just the shape you see, or the color, or the feel, or the way it moves in the breeze.


Now that brings us to HTML. So what are the semantics of a tag or an element? Does a browser need to ruminate on the deeper meaning of the <strong> element before interpreting the code?

Well, no...

But we do.

For illustrative purposes let's start with the most obvious set of tags that require us to pay some extra attention to the semantics of our HTML:

<i>, <b>, <em>, and <strong>

These are the italics, bold, emphasis, and strong tags.

Visually, there is no difference between the italics and emphasis tags or the bold and strong tags.

But as we know, there is more to meaning than what can be understood from any single dimension of perception. In fact, the <i> element can be used in a variety of ways that visually illustrate some minor degree of implied context, such as a tag line or motto, a quote from a reviewer, a subtitle to a book or movie, etc. This context only has meaning when perceived visually to the reader.

The <em> element, however, holds a more conversational meaning. As the name of the element suggests, it bestows a stressed emphasis on any text that it encapsulates. The nature of what is implied by that emphasis relies on the context of the rest of the sentence or document. When used with assistive technology like a screen reader, it indicates a stressed emphasis on its content, either by generating a special set of braille indicator symbols around the text, or by adding the stressed vocal quality to some, but not most voice screen readers.

And the difference between <b> and <strong> are very similar. Bolded text should be strictly used if you'd like to add a bit of formatting that acts as a visual support to the function of the content.

Strong text, on the other hand, should be used only to convey a sense of importance or urgency to a selection of text.
